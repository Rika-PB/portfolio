01 — Context & Challenge

"When a family member needs care, the last thing they should face is a confusing digital interface."

The problem was
emotional before it was
digital.

ecaria builds digital tools for people navigating Germany's complex care system — one of the most legislation-heavy, emotionally charged decision environments imaginable. The users aren't browsing casually. They're often in crisis.

The core challenge: complex legal care regulations, multiple decision paths, and a target audience of elderly users and families under high stress, most with limited digital experience — all navigating on a mobile device, often for the very first time.

A confusing interface here doesn't just cause frustration. It causes abandonment at exactly the moment a family most needs guidance. The UX had to earn trust before it asked for anything.

Target Audience

Elderly Users Primary

Often first-time smartphone users. Need large click targets, high contrast, plain language, and zero ambiguity at every step. Error states must feel safe, not punishing.

Family Caregivers Primary

Managing care decisions while also managing their own lives. High cognitive load, limited patience for anything unclear. Need fast orientation, confident guidance, and reassurance built into every flow.

Low Digital Literacy Universal

Design for this group and you design for everyone. No assumed knowledge, no jargon, no hidden affordances. The interface has to be self-explanatory — no onboarding document will be read.

02 — Design Principles

Three rules that governed
every single decision.

01 WCAG 2.1

One decision
per screen.

Never ask a user to process two things at once. Every screen presents a single question with a single clear action. Branching logic happens in the IA, invisibly — the user only ever sees the next step.

Guided Flow Principle

02 AA+

Large targets.
Always.

Minimum 44×44px touch targets across every interactive element — not just buttons. Tap areas extend beyond visible borders. For users with reduced motor precision, every missed tap is a broken experience.

Motor Accessibility

03 Plain Language

Plain language.
No jargon.

Every label, heading, and instruction was reviewed against plain language standards and tested with real caregivers. Legal care terminology was rewritten into human language without losing legal accuracy.

03 — The Process

Designed by listening.
Refined by testing.

01

User Research & Caregiver Interviews

I started by interviewing actual caregivers — not proxies or personas built from assumptions. The sessions revealed that the primary barrier wasn't digital literacy: it was emotional overwhelm. Users weren't failing to understand the interface; they were too stressed to focus on it. That insight reframed the entire design problem.

I also mapped Germany's Pflegegrad legislation to understand where decision complexity actually lives, and which parts could be simplified versus which required legal precision.

Caregiver Interviews Legislation Mapping Empathy Mapping

Key Finding

The barrier was emotional, not digital.

02

Guided Flow IA

The information architecture was built on a single governing constraint: one decision per screen. Complex care pathway logic was handled entirely in the IA layer — users never see branching or conditional logic. They only ever see the next, most relevant question for their situation.

This required mapping every possible user pathway through the Pflegegrad assessment system and designing a tree structure that felt linear even when it wasn't. Each branch had to feel equally calm and equally guided.

Decision Tree Mapping Linear Flow Design Pathway Architecture

Design Rule

Complexity lives in the IA. Never on screen.

03

Accessibility-First Wireframes

Wireframes were built with accessibility constraints active from the start — not applied retrospectively. Every interactive element was sized for WCAG 2.1 AA minimum tap targets (44×44px). Content hierarchy followed a strict single H1 per screen rule. Form elements had visible labels — no placeholder-as-label shortcuts.

I tested wireframes with actual elderly users early, specifically to identify where language caused hesitation — not just where buttons were hard to tap. Language revision happened at wireframe stage, not after visual design.

WCAG 2.1 AA Low-fi User Testing Language Validation

Approach

A11y from frame one. Never retrofitted.

04

Calm UI Design

The visual design had one primary brief: do not add to the user's stress. High contrast without harshness. Generous white space that signals breathing room, not emptiness. A muted colour palette with a single calm accent. No decorative elements that compete with content. Typography sized for readability, not aesthetic minimalism.

Progress indicators were designed to feel encouraging rather than pressurising — showing how far the user had come, not how far they had left to go.

High Contrast UI Calm Visual Rhythm Accessible Typography

Visual Principle

The interface shouldn't add to the stress.

05

Prototyping & Mobile Testing

Interactive Figma prototypes were built for both Pflegegradkompass and CareCademy — the two core products. Testing was conducted mobile-first, with real users on their own devices in realistic conditions. Not in a usability lab. In their kitchens.

Each test session focused on whether users could complete the flow without assistance. "Self-explanatory" was the success criterion — not "task completion rate with 1 hint". If a user paused to think, that was a redesign trigger.

Success Criterion

Zero assistance needed to complete the flow.

04 — Accessibility Deep-Dive

Accessibility wasn't a checklist.
It was the brief.

When your users are elderly, stressed, or digitally inexperienced — WCAG compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. Here's what that looked like in practice.

Touch Target Sizing

2.5.5 AAA

Every interactive element — buttons, links, form fields, navigation items — was built to a minimum 44×44px tap target. Tap areas were extended with invisible padding beyond visible borders, so users with tremor or reduced precision don't miss their intended target.

Min: 44×44px Ideal: 48×48px Gap between: 8px+

Colour Contrast

1.4.3 AA

All text/background combinations were tested against WCAG 4.5:1 minimum ratio for normal text and 3:1 for large text. No information was conveyed by colour alone — status indicators always paired colour with an icon or text label, supporting users with colour blindness.

Body: 7:1+ Interactive: 4.5:1+ Focus: 3:1+

Plain Language

3.1.5 AAA

Every piece of copy was reviewed against plain language principles. Legal Pflegegrad terminology was rewritten into everyday German without losing legal accuracy. Active voice, short sentences, concrete verbs. Instructions were validated in testing sessions — if users paused to re-read, the copy was rewritten.

Reading level: B1 or below

Error Prevention

3.3.4 AA

For users unfamiliar with digital forms, errors feel like failure. Every form was designed to prevent errors before they happen: inline validation with clear, non-judgmental language; no irreversible actions without explicit confirmation; progress never lost on back-navigation.

Back-nav: always preserves input

Focus Management

2.4.3 AA

On each screen transition, focus was deliberately managed to land on the primary heading or first interactive element — not left stranded on a nav item or lost off-screen. Keyboard and screen reader users received the same logical flow as pointer users.

Focus order: top to bottom, logical

Visible Progress

UX Principle

Progress indicators were designed to reinforce confidence, not create pressure. Showing steps completed rather than steps remaining. Language: "You've answered 4 questions" not "6 questions left." Small linguistic choices that make a material difference to a user already under emotional strain.

Framing: achievement, not distance

06 — Reflection

What designing for
vulnerability taught me
about design in general.

ecaria fundamentally changed how I approach every project. Designing for users at their most stressed and least digitally confident forces a level of clarity discipline that most design contexts don't demand. When something is unclear here, it doesn't cause mild frustration — it causes abandonment at a moment of genuine need.

The "one decision per screen" principle I developed for ecaria has since influenced how I approach information architecture in every context — including data-dense SaaS. The question is always the same: what is the one thing this screen needs to communicate?

And accessibility stopped being a compliance concern the moment I sat with an elderly woman who said, quietly, that she'd given up on the previous tool because she kept pressing the wrong button. That's who we design for.

Key Takeaways

01

Constraints produce clarity

The tightest constraints — one decision per screen, no jargon, 44px targets — produced the clearest design. Restrictions aren't limitations; they're design direction.

02

Test with the actual user

No persona or proxy reveals what sitting with a real 74-year-old user reveals in five minutes. The gap between assumed and actual user behaviour closes only with direct observation.

03

Accessibility is UX quality

Every accessibility improvement — clearer language, larger targets, better focus management — also improved the experience for users with no accessibility needs. They're not separate concerns.

04

Calm is a design decision

Visual calm is achievable through intention: generous space, a single accent, muted palette, unhurried typography. You don't need colour or animation to communicate confidence — you need restraint.
